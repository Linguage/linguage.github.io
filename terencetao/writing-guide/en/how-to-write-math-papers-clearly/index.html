How to write math papers clearly

Writing a mathematical paper is both an act of recording mathematical content and a means of communication of one‚Äôs work. In contrast with other types of writing, the style of math papers is incredibly rigid and resistant to even modest innovation. As a result, both goals suffer, sometimes immeasurably. The clarity suffers the most, which affects everyone in the field.

Over the years, I have been giving advice to my students and postdocs on how to write clearly. I collected them all in these notes. Please consider reading them and passing them to your students and colleagues.

Below I include one subsection dealing with different reference styles and what each version really means. This is somewhat subjective, of course. Enjoy!

****
4.2. How to cite a single paper. The citation rules are almost as complicated as Chinese honorifics, with an added disadvantage of never being discussed anywhere. Below we go through the (incomplete) list of possible ways in the decreasing level of citation importance and/or proof reliability.

(1) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture in [Roth].‚Äù Clear.

(2) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture [Roth].‚Äù Roth proved the conjecture, possibly in a different paper, but this is likely a definitive version of the proof.

(3) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture, see [Roth].‚Äù Roth proved the conjecture, but [Roth] can be anything from the original paper to the followup, to some kind of survey Roth wrote. Very occasionally you have ‚Äú see [Melville]‚Äù, but that usually means that Roth‚Äôs proof is unpublished or otherwise unavailable (say, it was given at a lecture, and Roth can‚Äôt be bothered to write it up), and Melville was the first to publish Roth‚Äôs proof, possibly without permission, but with attribution and perhaps filling some minor gaps.

(4) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture [Roth], see also [Woolf].‚Äù Apparently Woolf also made an important contribution, perhaps extending it to greater generality, or fixing some major gaps or errors in [Roth].

(5) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture in [Roth] (see also [Woolf]).‚Äù Looks like [Woolf] has a complete proof of Roth, possibly fixing some minor errors in [Roth].

(6) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture (see [Woolf]).‚Äù Here [Woolf] is a definitive version of the proof, e.g. the standard monograph on the subject.

(7) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture, see e.g. [Faulkner, Fitzgerald, Frost].‚Äù The result is important enough to be cited and its validity confirmed in several books/surveys. If there ever was a controversy whether Roth‚Äôs argument is an actual proof, it was resolved in Roth‚Äôs favor. Still, the original proof may have been too long, incomplete or simply presented in an old fashioned way, or published in an inaccessible conference proceedings, so here are sources with a better or more recent exposition. Or, more likely, the author was too lazy to look for the right reference, so overcompensated with three random textbooks on the subject.

(8) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture (see e.g. [Faulkner, Fitzgerald, Frost]).‚Äù The result is probably classical or at least very well known. Here are books/surveys which all probably have statements and/or proofs. Neither the author nor the reader will ever bother to check.

(9) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture.7 Footnote 7: See [Mailer].‚Äù Most likely, the author never actually read [Mailer], nor has access to that paper. Or, perhaps, [Mailer] states that Roth proved the conjecture, but includes neither a proof nor a reference. The author cannot
verify the claim independently and is visibly annoyed by the ambiguity, but felt obliged to credit Roth for the benefit of the reader, or to avoid the wrath of Roth.

(10) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture.7 Footnote 7: Love letter from H. Fielding to J. Austen, dated December 16, 1975.‚Äù This means that the letter likely exists and contains the whole proof or at least an outline of the proof. The author may or may not have seen it. Googling will probably either turn up the letter or a public discussion about what‚Äôs in it, and why it is not available.

(11) ‚Äú Roth proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture.7 Footnote 7: Personal communication.‚Äù This means Roth has sent the author an email (or said over beer), claiming to have a proof. Or perhaps Roth‚Äôs student accidentally mentioned this while answering a question after the talk. The proof
may or may not be correct and the paper may or may not be forthcoming.

(12) ‚Äú Roth claims to have proved Murakami‚Äôs conjecture in [Roth].‚Äù Paper [Roth] has a well known gap which was never fixed even though Roth insists on it to be fixable; the author would rather avoid going on record about this, but anything is possible after some wine at a banquet. Another possibility is that [Roth] is completely erroneous as explained elsewhere, but Roth‚Äôs
work is too famous not to be mentioned; in that case there is often a followup sentence clarifying the matter, sometimes in parentheses as in ‚Äú(see, however, [Atwood])‚Äù. Or, perhaps, [Roth] is a 3 page note published in Doklady Acad. Sci. USSR back in the 1970s, containing a very brief outline of the proof, and despite considerable effort nobody has yet to give a complete proof of its Lemma 2; there wouldn‚Äôt be any followup to this sentence then, but the author would be happy to clarify things by email.
